Term: Sport Mode
Definition: Sport Mode on a drone, especially DJI models, is a flight mode designed for maximum speed and maneuverability. When activated, the drone's motors run at higher speeds, increasing its overall velocity by about 30% compared to standard modes1. The controller gain is also heightened, making the drone more responsive to stick movements, allowing for sharper turns and quicker directional changes.

However, obstacle avoidance systems are disabled in Sport Mode, making it less suitable for beginners or flying in cluttered environments. GPS stabilization remains active, ensuring the drone maintains its position and altitude1.

In essence, Sport Mode is perfect for experienced pilots looking to perform high-speed maneuvers and stunts, but it comes with increased risks of crashes if not handled carefully.

Term: Positioning Mode
Definition: Positioning Mode, often referred to as P-Mode, is a standard flight mode found in many DJI drones. When activated, the drone uses GPS and vision sensors to maintain its position and stability. This mode enables features like obstacle avoidance and automatic return-to-home functions, making it highly suitable for beginners and general use.

In Positioning Mode, the drone can perform standard flying operations, such as filming, photography, and basic maneuvers, with enhanced safety due to the active stabilization systems. The vertical maximum height is typically 500 meters, and there's no horizontal limit, allowing for greater freedom of movement.

This mode is ideal for those who want a balance of control and safety while flying their drone.

Term: Attitude Mode
Definition: Attitude Mode (often abbreviated as ATTI Mode) is a flight mode where the drone operates without the assistance of GPS or Vision Positioning Systems (VPS). In this mode, the pilot has full manual control over the drone's movements, making it necessary to constantly adjust the controls to maintain stability and position.

Here are some key points about Attitude Mode:

Manual Control: The pilot must manually control the drone's pitch, roll, yaw, and throttle.

No GPS or VPS: Without GPS or VPS, the drone won't automatically stabilize itself or return to home.

Barometer-Based Altitude: The drone uses a barometer to maintain its altitude, but it can drift due to wind or other factors.

Use Cases: Ideal for indoor flying, flying in GPS-denied environments (like under dense foliage or near large metal structures), or for professional videographers who want smoother footage without automated corrections.

ATTI Mode requires skill and practice, as it offers no automatic corrections or obstacle avoidance. It's often used in situations where GPS signals are unreliable or for specific professional applications.

Term: LOS (Line Of Sight)

Unobstructed View: The pilot should have an unobstructed view of the drone at all times, without relying on any visual aids.

Technological Aids
While LOS is primarily about the pilot's unaided vision, technology like FPV (First-Person View) cameras can supplement situational awareness but should not replace LOS.

Challenges and Solutions
Distance: As the drone gets farther away, it becomes harder to maintain LOS. Pilots should be aware of their limits and plan flights accordingly.

Obstructions: Trees, buildings, and other structures can obstruct LOS. Pilots should choose flight paths that minimize these obstacles.

Maintaining LOS is a fundamental aspect of safe and responsible drone operation, ensuring that the pilot can respond promptly to any situation that may arise.

Term: RTH (Return to Home)
Definition: Return to Home (RTH) is a safety feature found in many modern drones, designed to automatically guide the drone back to a pre-determined home point. Here's an expert's perspective on this essential function:

Key Functions of Return to Home (RTH)
Types of RTH:

Smart RTH: Activated manually by the pilot via a button on the remote controller or app. The drone returns to the home point using GPS navigation.

Failsafe RTH: Automatically triggered if the drone loses connection with the remote controller for a certain period or if the battery level becomes critically low.

Low Battery RTH: Initiated when the drone detects that the battery level is too low to continue safe flight, ensuring it has enough power to return.

Home Point Setting:

The home point is usually set automatically as the location where the drone takes off, but it can also be manually set via the remote controller or app.

Drones with advanced GPS capabilities can update the home point dynamically during flight, based on the pilot's location or the drone's current position.

RTH Procedure:

Ascent: The drone first ascends to a specified altitude to avoid obstacles.

Navigation: It then flies in a straight line towards the home point.

Descent and Landing: Finally, the drone descends and lands safely at or near the home point.

Obstacle Avoidance:

Many drones equipped with obstacle detection systems can avoid obstacles during the RTH process. However, this feature depends on the drone model and the environment.

Benefits of RTH
Safety: Ensures the drone returns safely in case of signal loss or low battery, reducing the risk of crashes or loss.

Ease of Use: Provides peace of mind for pilots, especially beginners, knowing that the drone can autonomously return home if needed.

Operational Efficiency: Allows for efficient retrieval of the drone, minimizing downtime and ensuring the pilot can focus on other tasks.

Considerations
Environmental Factors: RTH performance can be affected by strong winds, GPS interference, or obstacles not detected by the drone's sensors.

Altitude Settings: Properly setting the RTH altitude is crucial to ensure the drone can clear any obstacles on its return path.

Overall, the Return to Home feature is a vital safety mechanism that enhances the reliability and user-friendliness of drone operations.

Term: RTK (Real-Time Kinematic)
Definition: From a drone expert's perspective, Real-Time Kinematic (RTK) is a satellite navigation technique used to enhance the accuracy of GPS and GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite System) positioning. RTK provides centimeter-level precision by using a fixed base station to correct the position data received by the mobile receiver, such as a drone12.

Here's how RTK works:

Base Station: A stationary receiver placed at a known, fixed location continuously receives GNSS signals and calculates corrections based on its known position2.
Rover: The mobile receiver (drone) also receives GNSS signals and uses the correction data from the base station to determine its precise position2.
Communication Link: A data link (radio, cellular, or internet) transmits correction data from the base station to the rover in real-time2.
RTK is crucial for tasks that demand high-accuracy location data, such as:

Surveying: Ensuring precise measurements and mapping1.
Construction: Accurate positioning for site planning and monitoring1.
Agriculture: Precision farming techniques like planting and spraying1.
By reducing positional errors, RTK enables drones to perform detailed and accurate surveys, ensuring high-quality data collection and operational efficiency2.

Advanced Features: Supports features like obstacle avoidance, waypoint navigation, and autonomous flight by providing reliable and accurate measurements1.

Term: Pitch
Definition: From a drone expert's perspective, Pitch refers to the angle of the propeller blades relative to their base plate. This angle determines how much air the propeller pushes down with each rotation, which in turn affects the drone's lift and thrust12.

Here's a detailed look at pitch:

Definition: Pitch is measured in inches and represents the theoretical distance a propeller would move forward in one complete rotation if it were moving through a solid medium1.
Impact on Performance: A higher pitch means the blades are more angled, producing more thrust and potentially higher speeds. However, it also requires more power and can generate more turbulence2.
Application: The right pitch depends on the specific use case. For example, racing drones often use high-pitch propellers for speed, while photography drones might use lower-pitch propellers for stability and smooth flight2.
Understanding propeller pitch is crucial for optimizing drone performance and ensuring efficient and safe flight operations.

Term: Throttle
Definition: From a drone expert's perspective, Throttle is a crucial parameter that controls the vertical movement of a drone by adjusting the power supplied to its motors. Here's a detailed look at how throttle works:

Vertical Control: Throttle directly influences the drone's altitude. Increasing the throttle raises the drone by increasing the motor speed, which generates more lift. Conversely, decreasing the throttle lowers the drone by reducing the motor speed12.
Hovering: To maintain a stable hover, the throttle must be set at a level where the lift generated by the propellers equals the drone's weight. This is known as the hover throttle1.
Maneuverability: The throttle setting affects the drone's ability to perform maneuvers. For instance, a higher throttle setting provides more power for quick ascents and agile movements, while a lower setting is used for gentle descents and stable hovering2.
Understanding throttle is essential for precise control and safe operation of the drone, especially during takeoff, landing, and altitude adjustments.

Term: Yaw
Definition: From a drone expert's perspective, Yaw refers to the rotational movement of a drone around its vertical axis. This movement allows the drone to turn left or right while maintaining its altitude and position12.

Here's a detailed look at yaw:

Definition: Yaw is the rotation around the vertical axis, which runs perpendicular to the drone's body. This axis is often referred to as the z-axis2.
Control: Yaw is typically controlled by varying the speed of the drone's motors. For example, increasing the speed of the motors on one side of the drone while decreasing the speed on the opposite side causes the drone to rotate around its vertical axis1.
Applications: Yaw is essential for tasks that require precise orientation, such as aerial photography, videography, and navigation. It allows the drone to change its direction without altering its position or altitude2.
Understanding yaw is crucial for mastering drone flight and achieving smooth, controlled maneuvers.

Term: Roll
Definition: From a drone expert's perspective, Roll refers to the rotational movement of a drone around its longitudinal axis, which runs from the front (nose) to the back (tail) of the drone12. This movement allows the drone to tilt to the left or right, enabling it to change direction or maintain stability during flight.

Here's a detailed look at roll:

Definition: Roll is the rotation around the longitudinal axis, causing one wing or side of the drone to dip while the opposite side rises2.
Control: Roll is typically controlled by adjusting the speed of the motors on either side of the drone. For example, increasing the speed of the motors on the left side while decreasing the speed on the right side causes the drone to roll to the right1.
Applications: Roll is essential for maneuvering the drone, especially when making turns or maintaining balance in windy conditions. It is also crucial for performing aerial stunts and precise navigation2.
Understanding roll is vital for mastering drone flight and achieving smooth, controlled maneuvers.
